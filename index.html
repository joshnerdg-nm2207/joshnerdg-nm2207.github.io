Religiousness and Perceived God Perfectionism Among Elite Athletes


The study aims to provide a better understanding of the relationship between religious beliefs and athletic performance under pressure.


Scholars from various fields have analysed and synthesised the integration of religion and sports historically and presently.

However, research on the impact of religious practices and experiences on athletes' emotional health and performance in sports is limited.

The little research that exists has yielded mixed results, and further investigation is necessary due to the established link between religiousness and psychological well-being in other fields of psychology.

The study focuses on the correlation between religiousness, perceptions of God perfectionism, and how elite athletes cope with disappointing performance and upcoming competitions.

Researchers analysed the cognitive appraisals of athletes and their reactions towards God during low performance, and linked these findings to the athletes' global self worth and perfectionistic concerns.

Keywords: Religion, God Perfectionism, Self-Views, Elite Athlete, and Coping Strategies

Premise 1
The Promise of Religiousness: Promotion of Global Self Worth

Definitions:
Religion is defined as a system of beliefs and practices observed by a community, supported by rituals that acknowledge the Sacred, Divine, God or Ultimate Truth.

Religiousness is measured by engagement in both public and private religious activities, as well as personal religious commitment and motivation.

Premise 1
The Promise of Religiousness: Promotion of Global Self Worth

Elaboration:
Religious beliefs can benefit athletes by promoting self-worth and improving emotional well-being, allowing them to better cope with challenges. In elite sports, where self-worth is often tied to performance, religious beliefs provide a more stable and consistent source of self-worth based on faith, which is important in a culture with unhealthy self-views.

Premise 2
The Pitfall of Religion: Views of God as Perfectionistic

Definition:
God perfectionism refers to the belief among some religious athletes that their performance is tied to God's love or affection. This can create pressure to achieve perfect performance in order to prove their worth and stay in God's favour. It is characterised by a view of God as highly perfectionistic.

Premise 2
The Pitfall of Religion: Views of God as Perfectionistic

Elaboration:
Perfectionism can have adaptive and maladaptive aspects. Setting high standards can be beneficial, but if they are unattainable, it can lead to negative outcomes. Beliefs about God's perfectionism can also affect personal perfectionism. For elite athletes, a high discrepancy between God's standards and their own performance can lead to negative outcomes, including perfectionistic concern.

Premise 3
How Views of God Affect Appraisals of Performance: Looking to the Past and Future

Although religiousness and views of God’s perfectionism are conceptualised as stable dispositions, the ways they are expressed and affect the emotional health outcomes of athletes may differ depending on the relevant situation. Two types of psychological situations that are highly salient for appraisals of self-worth and perfectionism are dealing with:

1. Disappointing Performances

2. Anticipating an upcoming competition

Disappointing performances

Athletes dealing with disappointing performances can either internalise failure as a reflection of their worth or use it as an opportunity for growth. Shame can arise from this failure and lead to negative emotions.

Turning towards God can lead to better psychological adjustment and comfort where higher levels of intrinsic religiousness are linked to more positive religious and spiritual coping.

Anticipating an upcoming competition

Athletes in high-pressure situations can experience stress related to cognitive appraisals, such as viewing competition as a threat or a challenge.

Challenge appraisals are linked to positive coping skills and performance satisfaction, while threat appraisals are linked to negative emotions and maladaptive coping.

Study:

Physiological Situation: Disappointing performances

Hypothesis 1: Athlete religiousness and perceptions of God’s perfectionism will be directly related to athletes’ attitudes towards God (both comfort from & anger towards) and feelings of shame after recalling a failed sporting performance.

Hypothesis 2: Religiousness and God perfectionism will be indirectly related to shame through self-worth and perfectionistic concern.

Study:

Physiological Situation: Anticipating an upcoming competition

Hypothesis 3: Religiousness and God perfectionism will only be related to cognitive stress appraisals (challenge, stress, threat) of an upcoming competition through their relationship with self-worth and perfectionistic concerns.

Strategic Analysis

The study used descriptive statistics and bivariate correlations to analyse the variables. Structural equation modelling (SEM) was then conducted to test two separate physiological situations.

Both models included a latent factor for religiousness with three indicators, as well as observed variables of global self-worth and perfectionistic concern.

Legend: NORA = Non-organisational religious activity; ORA = Organisational religious activity; IR = Intrinsic Religiousness; GPS = God perfectionism standards; GPD = God perfectionism discrepancy; RSES = Global self-worth; PERF = Perfectionism Concern; COM = Feelings of comfort from God; CA = Challenge appraisal; SA = Stress appraisal; TA = Threat appraisal

Results:
Disappointing performances

An athlete's level of religiousness and perfectionistic views of God (discrepancy and standards) were directly related to their attitude towards God after experiencing a disappointing performance in different ways.

Specifically, religiousness was related to feelings of comfort from God, whereas high levels of both standards and discrepancy were related to higher levels of anger towards God.

Religiousness and discrepancy were also indirectly related to shame through self-worth and perfectionistic concerns.

Perceived God standards were not related to self-worth, perfectionistic concern, or shame.

Results:
Anticipating an upcoming competition

Religiousness and perceived God discrepancy were indirectly related to appraisals of upcoming competition but in different ways.

Higher levels of reported religiousness were related to viewing an upcoming competition as a challenge and negatively related to both threat and stress appraisals.

Meanwhile, perceived God discrepancy was linked to challenge appraisals via a negative relation with self-worth.

Additionally, perceived God perfectionism discrepancy was indirectly related to both threat and stress appraisals of upcoming competitions through a positive relation to perfectionistic concerns.

Findings:
The study found that athletes' religiousness and perceived discrepancy from God were related to their post-disappointment shame and appraisals of upcoming competition through their impact on global self-worth and perfectionistic concerns.

Religiousness was linked to higher levels of self-worth and comfort from God, whereas perceived discrepancy from God was linked to lower levels of self-worth and higher levels of anger towards God.

Additionally, religiousness was related to viewing competition as a challenge through a positive relationship with global self-worth. The results were consistent even when years of competition were taken into account, and no further analyses were conducted due to power constraints.

Conclusion:
The study underscores the importance of addressing the religious and spiritual needs of elite athletes in their mental preparation for performance.

Practitioners may require cultural competence in understanding the impact of religiosity/spirituality on coping.

Engaging in conversations about athletes' religious faith can strengthen rapport and understanding.

It is crucial to challenge the view of performance-based self-worth and help athletes find value in their religious beliefs.

Highlighting the emotional and spiritual disruption caused by perfectionistic views of God and emphasizes the need for healthy religious identity formation among athletes.
